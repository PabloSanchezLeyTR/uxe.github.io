Summary

Under New York law, a plaintiff can maintain a failure to hire discrimination claim even when the employer hires someone of the same race or gender as the plaintiff. While hiring someone from the same protected class as the plaintiff is evidence that weighs against an inference of discrimination, it is not an absolute defense [1]. Additionally, even if an employer hires someone of the same race or gender as the plaintiff, a discrimination claim may still proceed based on other protected characteristics [2] [3]. Recent amendments to the New York State Human Rights Law (NYSHRL) require liberal construction to accomplish its anti-discriminatory purposes, making it more employee-friendly than federal law [4].

New York employment discrimination law framework

Employment discrimination claims in New York are primarily governed by the New York State Human Rights Law (NYSHRL), codified in Executive Law § 296. NYSHRL prohibits discrimination in employment based on protected characteristics including race, color, national origin, sex, age, disability, sexual orientation, gender identity, and other protected classes, Executive Law § 296 [5].

To establish a prima facie case of discrimination under the NYSHRL, a plaintiff must demonstrate:

  1. Membership in a protected class
  2. Qualification for the position
  3. An adverse employment action (such as failure to hire)
  4. Circumstances giving rise to an inference of discrimination Blackman v. Metro. Transit Auth., 206 A.D.3d 602, 169 N.Y.S.3d 653 (2022)[6]

This framework generally mirrors the federal Title VII framework established in McDonnell Douglas Corp v. Green [7]. Historically, New York courts have applied the same standards for NYSHRL claims as federal courts apply to Title VII claims [8]. However, recent amendments to the NYSHRL have made it more employee-friendly, requiring liberal construction to maximize deterrent effects of the law [9].

Same protected class defense in failure to hire cases not an absolute defense

The central question is whether a plaintiff can maintain a failure to hire discrimination claim when the employer ultimately hires someone of the same protected class. Based on New York case law, the fact that an employer hired someone of the same protected class as the plaintiff is not an absolute defense to a discrimination claim [10] [11].

While not an absolute defense, courts do consider the hiring of someone from the same protected class as evidence weighing against an inference of discrimination [12]. In Ruderman v. New York State Insurance Fund, the court noted that the fact that NYSIF "ultimately selected candidates from a broad age range, including at least one candidate who was the same age as plaintiff" was a factor that "weigh[ed] against any inference of discrimination" [13].

One factor among many

Courts consider the hiring of someone from the same protected class as one factor among many in determining whether discrimination occurred [18]. As the court in Blackman demonstrated, the focus is on whether the employer's stated reasons for the hiring decision were pretextual for discrimination, not simply on the protected characteristics of the person who was hired [19].

Importantly, even if an employer hires someone of the same race or gender as the plaintiff, a discrimination claim may still proceed based on other protected characteristics [20]. In Ayers v. Bloomberg, L.P., while the sex discrimination claim was dismissed where another woman was hired, the age discrimination claim was allowed to proceed [21].

Recent developments in New York law

Recent amendments to the NYSHRL have made it more employee-friendly, similar to the New York City Human Rights Law (NYCHRL) [22]. The state law now requires liberal construction to accomplish its anti-discriminatory purposes, regardless of how similar federal statutes are interpreted [23]. As stated in Executive Law § 300:

"The provisions of this article shall be construed liberally for the accomplishment of the remedial purposes thereof, regardless of whether federal civil rights laws, including those laws with provisions worded comparably to the provisions of this article, have been so construed." [24]

The amendments also specify that exceptions and exemptions to the NYSHRL must be narrowly construed [25].

Practical implications

From a practical standpoint, while hiring someone of the same protected class as the plaintiff can be helpful evidence for an employer defending against a discrimination claim, it is not dispositive [26] [27].

Plaintiffs alleging discrimination despite the hiring of someone from the same protected class should focus on:

  1. Evidence of pretext in the employer's stated reasons for the hiring decision
  2. Discriminatory comments or conduct during the hiring process
  3. Disparate treatment compared to similarly situated candidates
  4. Statistical evidence showing patterns of discrimination
  5. Discrimination based on other protected characteristics

Employers, on the other hand, should not rely solely on the fact that they hired someone from the same protected class as the plaintiff. They should ensure that their hiring decisions are based on legitimate, non-discriminatory criteria and that they maintain thorough documentation of the hiring process.

Arguments

Arguments against the viability of a discrimination claim:

  1. Inference against discrimination:
    • The hiring of someone within the same protected class creates a strong inference against discriminatory intent, as consistently seen in cases like Meyer v. State of New York Office of Mental Health, 174 F.Supp.3d 673 (E.D.N.Y. 2016) and Rodriguez v. New York City Health and Hospitals Corp., 2015 WK 5229850 (2015).
  2. Legitimate non-discriminatory reasons:
    • Employers can often provide legitimate, non-discriminatory reasons for their hiring decisions, such as superior qualifications or better fit for the role, as demonstrated in Gautam v. Prudential Financial, Inc., 2008 WL 11417411 (E.D.N.Y. 2008) and Johnson v. Dynamic Educational Systems, Inc.. 2011 WL 2635444 (E.D.N.Y. 2011).

Arguments supporting the viability of a discrimination claim:

  1. Members of a protected class aren't identical. One member can be treated in a discriminatory manner based on that class, even if others aren't, as shown in cases like Phillips v. Chertoff, 2005 WL 3466033 (S.D.N.Y. 2005).
    • For instance, an employee of a certain race with a strong accent is denied a certain promotion, while another employee of the same race - but without any accent - is granted that promotion. Although both employees fall into the same protected class, the employer's different treatment of the two could still have been due to race.
    • Similarly, the ADEA protects employees over the age of 40. But an employer could still deny a promotion to a 60-year-old based on age, even though they give the promotion to a 40-year-old. Although both fall in the same protected class, the employer's different treatment could still have been due to age.
    • Whether unlawful discrimination occurred in these or other situations therefore depends on the facts of each specific case.

Jurisdictional differences

The 9th Circuit has held that the inference of discrimination is not dependent on an examination of who, if anyone, was promoted instead of the plaintiff. Because Title VII was designed to protect individuals in addition to groups, a plaintiff is not precluded from suing merely because a person from the same protected class was selected for the position. Diaz v. Am. Tel. & Tel., 752 F.2d 1356 (9th Cir. 1985).

Unusual situations

If a plaintiff is replaced with a member of their protected class after the filing of a discrimination charge, the facts might suggest pretext or a cover-up. Fleming v. MaxMara USA, Inc., 371 F. App'x 115 (2d Cir. 2010) (discussing cases that held a cover-up may exist when the plaintiff is replaced with a member of her protected class after the filing of a discrimination charge).

There could be a jury issue as to whether the person hired for the position is a member of the same protected class as the plaintiff or not. Barrella v. Vill. of Freeport, 43 F. Supp. 3d 136, 178 (E.D.N.Y. 2014) (reviewing jury decision regarding the race of the person hired for the position in a failure to promote case).

Related issues

Retailiation

Even if the plaintiff cannot prove discriminatory motive for the refusal to hire, she may still prevail by showing the employer refused to hire her because she previously complained about discrimination or engaged in other protected conduct. Retaliation has its own causation test and may survive when the underlying discrimination count falters. Harriram v. City Univ. of New York, No. 22-CV-9712 (RA), 2024 WL 1374804, at *7 (S.D.N.Y. Mar. 31, 2024).

Same-class decision maker

When the decision-makers are also members of the plaintiff's protected class, this creates an additional inference against discrimination [27]. In Meyer, the court noted that when the decision-maker is in the same protected class(es) as the plaintiff-employee, courts can draw inferences against discriminatory intent. This may further complicate a plaintiff's ability to establish discriminatory intent in same-protected-class hiring decisions. Meyer v. State of New York Off. of Mental Health, 174 F. Supp. 3d 673, 688 (E.D.N.Y. 2016).
